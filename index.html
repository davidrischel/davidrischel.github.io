David Zapero

I am a postdoctoral researcher in political philosophy at the Institute of Philosophy, Goethe University Frankfurt. My work sits at the intersection of distributive justice, political legitimacy, and democratic theory.

I am currently on the academic job market.

My research addresses questions about territory, land, and housing — who has the right to control particular pieces of the earth, and on what terms. I approach these questions through the tradition of analytic political philosophy, drawing on and arguing with Rawlsian liberalism, Georgist political economy, and theories of political legitimacy.

Alongside this, I have a growing interest in the theory of democratic institutions. I am interested in what alternatives to standard electoral democracy might look like — citizens' assemblies, sortition, and related proposals — and how they can be justified from a liberal-egalitarian standpoint.

Before Frankfurt, I completed my PhD at the University of Warwick (Politics and International Studies), where I received the Sir Ernest Barker Prize nomination for my dissertation. I have held teaching positions at Queen Mary University of London and Goethe University Frankfurt.

Research

My work falls across three connected areas. The first concerns the foundations of territorial rights and the distributive justice of land. The second applies these foundations to questions of housing policy. The third addresses democratic theory, focusing on institutional alternatives to electoral representation.

Book project

Territory, Legitimacy, and Justice

My research monograph develops a liberal-egalitarian theory of territorial rights. The central argument is that the legitimacy of a state's claim to a territory cannot be separated from how justly it distributes access to that territory. Drawing on Georgist political economy, I argue that land value — as distinct from the value created by labour and capital — is a shared resource that any just society must treat as common wealth. The book defends this view against rival accounts grounded in nationalist, functionalist, and libertarian premises.

Territorial justice and land

Jurisdiction and distributive claims

A thread running through several papers concerns the relationship between a state's jurisdictional authority and its obligations to distribute resources fairly. I argue that these cannot be cleanly separated: a state that systematically excludes people from the land it controls undermines the very legitimacy conditions that ground its jurisdictional claims.

Georgism and liberal egalitarianism

I am interested in how the Georgist tradition — long marginalised in academic political philosophy — can be reconstructed on liberal-egalitarian foundations. Henry George's core insight about the unearned character of land rent survives the usual objections to his historical proposals, and connects productively to contemporary debates about luck egalitarianism, relational equality, and the ownership society.

Housing justice

The politics of land use and housing

Housing policy is among the most consequential applications of theories of land justice. I am working on how principles of territorial justice bear on questions of zoning, planning regulation, and the distribution of housing costs — with attention to what a genuinely just housing policy would require beyond standard affordability framings.

Democratic theory

Beyond electoral representation

A newer strand of my work examines proposals for reforming or supplementing electoral democracy — citizens' assemblies, sortition-based institutions, and deliberative mini-publics. I am interested in how these can be justified not merely on epistemic grounds but as expressions of a commitment to political equality, and what their limits are when assessed against liberal-democratic values.
